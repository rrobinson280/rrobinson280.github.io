A Visual Intuition for Space Filling Curves

Ryan Robinson

A space-filling curve can be thought of as a continuous mapping from a lower dimensional space to a higher one. This article attempts to provide visual intuition for this mapping as well as rigorous proof.

Let I=[0,1]. There exists a continuous map f:I→I2 whose image fills up the entire square I2.

Useful Theorems

Theorem 1 Let X be a topological space and let (Y,d) be a metric space. The set 𝒞⁢(X,Y) of continuous functions is closed in YX under the uniform metric. So is the set ℬ⁢(X,Y) of bounded functions. Therefore, if Y is complete, these spaces are complete in the uniform metric.

Uniform Limit Theorem. Let fn:X→Y be a sequence of continuous functions from the topological space X to the metric space Y. If (fn) converges uniformly to f, then f is continuous.

Proof

Proof.

Let fn:I→I2 be defined by the sequence of functions shown above and I=[0,1]. The first step will be to show that (fn) is a Cauchy sequence since then we can say that fn converges to a continuous function. After that we will show for any point x∈I2 then x∈f⁢(I).

First, consider the square metric on ℝ2,

d⁢(x,y)=max⁡{|x1−y1|,|x2−y2|}.

Then, let ρ be the sup metric corresponding to the square metric on the space of continuous maps 𝒞⁢(I,I2),

ρ⁢(f,g)=sup{d⁢(f⁢(t),g⁢(t))|t∈I}.

So, we now show that (fn) is Cauchy under ρ. Each operation makes an individual path in fn to lie in a square of length 1/2n+1. To turn fn into fn+1 we replace each path by four triangular paths that lie in the same 1/2n area. Then, the distance between fn⁢(t) and fn+1⁢(t) is at most 1/2n. Then ρ⁢(fn,fn+1)≤1/2n and (fn) is a Cauchy sequence since

ρ⁢(fn,fn+m)≤1/2n+1/2n+1+⋯+1/2n+m−1<2/2n

for all n and m.

Since ℝ2 is complete, every closed subset must also be complete. Then I2 is complete and 𝒞⁢(I,I2) must be complete under the uniform metric by Theorem 1 and therefore is complete under the sup metric.

Since fn:I→I2 is a Cauchy sequence on a complete space this sequence converges uniformly to f and is therefore continuous by the uniform limit theorem. So, we have shown that f exists and is continuous. Lastly, we show f is also surjective.

So, consider a point x∈I2, we must show that this point belongs to f⁢(I). Each fn comes within a distance of 1/2n of x since x must be in one of the squares that make up I2 belonging to a triangular path.

So, let ϵ>0 and choose N such that

ρ⁢(fN,f)<ϵ/2⁢ and ⁢1/2N<ϵ/2.

As peviously argued, there will be a point t0∈I where the distance between x and fN⁢(t0) is less than 1/2N. that is d⁢(x,fN⁢(t0))≤1/2N. Then, by how we have chosen N it follows that

d⁢(x,f⁢(t0))<ϵ,

so f⁢(I) will intersect the ϵ-neighborhood of x.

Therefore, we have shown that x∈f⁢(I)¯ since I is compact and f is continuous f⁢(I) must also be compact. Then f⁢(I) must be closed so f⁢(I)=f⁢(I)¯ and x∈f⁢(I). Therefore, we have shown that f is is a continuous map from I to I2 which fills I2.

∎
